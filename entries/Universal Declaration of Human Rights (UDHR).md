The **Universal Declaration of Human Rights (UDHR)** is a landmark document adopted by the United Nations General Assembly on **December 10, 1948** in Paris. It sets out fundamental human rights to be universally protected, serving as a common standard for all peoples and nations.

## Background

- Drafted in the aftermath of World War II and the Holocaust.  
- The UN established a Human Rights Commission chaired by **Eleanor Roosevelt**.  
- Adopted by the General Assembly at the Palais de Chaillot, Paris.  
- 48 countries voted in favor, none opposed, with 8 abstentions.  

## Structure

- **Preamble** – Declares the importance of human rights for freedom, justice, and peace.  
- **30 Articles** – Outline civil, political, economic, social, and cultural rights.  

## Key Principles

1. **Equality and Non‑Discrimination** – All humans are born free and equal in dignity and rights.  
2. **Right to Life, Liberty, and Security** – Protection against slavery, torture, and arbitrary detention.  
3. **Freedom of Thought, Expression, and Religion** – Safeguards individual conscience and speech.  
4. **Political Rights** – Right to participate in government and free elections.  
5. **Economic and Social Rights** – Right to work, education, health, and adequate living standards.  
6. **Cultural Rights** – Right to freely participate in community and cultural life.  

## Importance

- First international recognition of universal human rights.  
- Inspired binding treaties such as the **International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR)** and the **International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (ICESCR)**.  
- Forms the foundation of modern human rights law.  

## Legacy

* Translated into over 500 languages, making it the most translated document in the world.  
* Serves as a guiding framework for governments, NGOs, and activists.  
* Celebrated annually on **Human Rights Day (December 10)**.  

---

*The UDHR remains a cornerstone of international human rights, affirming the dignity and equality of all people.*
