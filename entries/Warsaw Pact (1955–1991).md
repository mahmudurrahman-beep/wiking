## Definition
The **Warsaw Pact**, officially the *Treaty of Friendship, Cooperation, and Mutual Assistance*, was a military alliance of communist states in Eastern Europe led by the Soviet Union. It was created in **1955** as a counterbalance to NATO.

## Origins
- Signed: **May 14, 1955**, in Warsaw, Poland.  
- Trigger: West Germany’s admission into **NATO**.  
- Purpose: To strengthen military cooperation among socialist states and secure Soviet influence in Eastern Europe.  

## Member States
- **Founding members (1955):** Soviet Union, Poland, East Germany, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, Romania, Bulgaria, Albania.  
- Albania withdrew in **1968** after the Soviet invasion of Czechoslovakia.  

## Structure
- Led by the **Soviet Union**, which dominated military and political decisions.  
- Headquarters: **Moscow, USSR**.  
- Integrated military command under Soviet leadership.  

## Key Events
- **1956 Hungarian Uprising** – Warsaw Pact forces crushed anti‑Soviet protests.  
- **1968 Prague Spring** – Soviet and allied troops invaded Czechoslovakia to halt liberal reforms.  
- **1980s** – Growing dissent and weakening cohesion as Eastern Bloc states sought independence.  

## Dissolution
- With the collapse of communist regimes in Eastern Europe, the Pact lost relevance.  
- Officially dissolved on **July 1, 1991**, in Prague.  

## Legacy
- Symbolized the Cold War division between East and West.  
- Seen as a tool of Soviet dominance rather than equal partnership.  
- Its end marked the decline of Soviet influence in Europe and the conclusion of the Cold War era.
