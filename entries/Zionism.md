## Definition
**Zionism** is a political and cultural movement advocating for the return of the Jewish people to their ancestral homeland and the establishment of a sovereign Jewish state. The term comes from **Zion**, a biblical synonym for Jerusalem.

## Origins
- Founded in the late **19th century** in Europe.  
- Response to rising **antisemitism** and Jewish persecution.  
- **Theodor Herzl**, considered the father of modern Zionism, published *Der Judenstaat* (1896), calling for a Jewish state.  
- The **First Zionist Congress** (1897, Basel, Switzerland) formally launched the movement.  

## Key Milestones
- **1917 – Balfour Declaration**: British support for a Jewish homeland in Palestine.  
- **1920s–30s**: Jewish immigration to Palestine increased under British Mandate.  
- **1948 – Establishment of Israel**: Zionist efforts culminated in the creation of the State of Israel.  
- **Post‑1948**: Zionism evolved into supporting and strengthening Israel as the Jewish homeland.  

## Variants of Zionism
- **Political Zionism** – Focused on statehood and diplomacy.  
- **Labor Zionism** – Emphasized socialism and collective farming (kibbutzim).  
- **Revisionist Zionism** – Advocated for a more militant approach to statehood.  
- **Religious Zionism** – Saw the return to Israel as a divine mission.  

## Criticism and Debate
- Supporters view Zionism as a movement of **self‑determination** and survival.  
- Critics argue it displaced Palestinian communities and fueled conflict.  
- Today, debates continue over Zionism’s role in Israeli politics and global Jewish identity.  

## Legacy
- Zionism successfully established Israel as a Jewish homeland.  
- It remains central to Jewish identity and global politics.  
- Continues to shape Middle Eastern geopolitics and peace efforts.  

---

*Zionism is both a nationalist movement and a cultural identity, rooted in Jewish history and still shaping global debates today.*
