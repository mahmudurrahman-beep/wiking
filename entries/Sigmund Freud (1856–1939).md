## Definition
**Sigmund Freud** was an Austrian neurologist and the founder of **psychoanalysis**, a groundbreaking method for studying the human mind, unconscious processes, and mental disorders. His theories profoundly influenced psychology, psychiatry, literature, and culture.

## Early Life
- Born: **May 6, 1856**, in Freiberg (now Příbor, Czech Republic).  
- Studied medicine at the University of Vienna.  
- Specialized in neurology before turning to psychological research.  

## Major Contributions
- **Psychoanalysis** – A therapeutic method involving free association, dream interpretation, and analysis of unconscious conflicts.  
- **Theory of the Unconscious** – Proposed that much of human behavior is driven by unconscious desires and repressed memories.  
- **Psychosexual Stages of Development** – Suggested personality develops through stages (oral, anal, phallic, latency, genital).  
- **Id, Ego, and Superego** – Structural model of the psyche balancing instinct, reality, and morality.  
- **Dream Analysis** – Called dreams the “royal road to the unconscious.”  

## Key Works
- *The Interpretation of Dreams* (1900)  
- *Three Essays on the Theory of Sexuality* (1905)  
- *Civilization and Its Discontents* (1930)  

## Legacy
- Revolutionized the study of the mind and therapy.  
- Influenced art, literature, and philosophy.  
- Criticized for lack of scientific rigor, but remains a central figure in psychology’s history.  
- Died: **September 23, 1939**, in London, England.  

---

*Sigmund Freud reshaped our understanding of the human mind, introducing concepts of the unconscious and psychoanalysis that continue to spark debate and inspire thought today.*
