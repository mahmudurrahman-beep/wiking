## Definition
The **NPT** is an international treaty designed to stop the spread of nuclear weapons, encourage peaceful nuclear cooperation, and promote disarmament.

## Origins
- Signed: **July 1, 1968** in Washington, London, and Moscow.  
- Entered into force: **March 5, 1970**.  
- Extended indefinitely: **May 11, 1995**.  

## Membership
- **191 states parties** (nearly universal).  
- Recognized nuclear-weapon states: **United States, Russia, United Kingdom, France, China**.  
- Non-parties: **India, Israel, Pakistan, South Sudan**.  
- North Korea withdrew in **2003**.  

## Three Pillars
1. **Non-Proliferation** – Prevent spread of nuclear weapons beyond the five recognized states.  
2. **Disarmament** – Commitment to pursue nuclear disarmament.  
3. **Peaceful Uses** – Promote cooperation in nuclear energy for civilian purposes under IAEA safeguards.  

## Importance
- Considered the **cornerstone of global nuclear security**.  
- Provides framework for **IAEA inspections** and safeguards.  
- Enabled significant reductions in nuclear stockpiles since the Cold War.  

## Challenges
- Some states remain outside the treaty.  
- Slow progress on disarmament criticized by non-nuclear states.  
- Ongoing debates about compliance (e.g., Iran, North Korea).  

---

*The NPT remains vital for global stability, balancing nuclear restraint with peaceful energy cooperation.*
