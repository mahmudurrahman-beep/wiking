## Definition
The **Dalai Lama** is the spiritual leader of Tibetan Buddhism and traditionally regarded as the reincarnation of Avalokiteśvara, the Bodhisattva of Compassion. The title has been held by a line of Tibetan Buddhist monks since the 15th century.

## Current Dalai Lama
- **Tenzin Gyatso**, the **14th Dalai Lama**, born **July 6, 1935** in Taktser, Tibet.  
- Enthroned in **1940** after being recognized as the reincarnation of the 13th Dalai Lama.  
- Took full political power in **1950**, shortly before the Chinese occupation of Tibet.  
- Fled to India in **1959** after the failed Tibetan uprising, establishing the Tibetan government‑in‑exile in Dharamshala.  

## Teachings and Role
- Advocates for **nonviolence, compassion, and interfaith dialogue**.  
- Promotes the **Middle Way Approach**: genuine autonomy for Tibet within China, rather than full independence.  
- Global figure for **peace, human rights, and environmental responsibility**.  
- Awarded the **Nobel Peace Prize in 1989**.  

## Historical Lineage
- The Dalai Lama lineage began in the **15th century** with **Gedun Drub (1391–1474)**, recognized posthumously as the 1st Dalai Lama.  
- Successive Dalai Lamas have been central to Tibetan religious and political life.  

## Legacy
- The Dalai Lama remains a symbol of Tibetan identity and resilience.  
- His teachings have influenced millions worldwide, beyond Buddhism.  
- The future of the institution is uncertain, as China rejects the traditional reincarnation process.
